\subsection*{B. Recommended Replacement Abstract}

\begin{quote}
\textbf{
Sensitive data release is vulnerable to output-side privacy threats such as membership inference, attribute inference, and record linkage, creating a need for mechanisms with both formal guarantees and empirically verifiable utility--privacy tradeoffs.
We propose REAEDP, a differential privacy framework that combines entropy-calibrated histogram release with a synthetic-data release mechanism and attack-based evaluation.
First, we derive an explicit sensitivity bound for Shannon entropy, together with an extension to Renyi entropy, enabling calibrated differentially private release of histogram statistics.
Second, we introduce a synthetic-data mechanism \(F\) with a privacy-test structure and show that it satisfies \((\varepsilon,\delta)\)-differential privacy under the stated parameter domain.
Experiments on multiple public tabular datasets show that the theoretical entropy bound is respected empirically, standard Laplace and Gaussian baselines remain below the bound, and both membership-inference and linkage-style attacks move toward random-guess behavior as \(\varepsilon\) decreases.
These results indicate that REAEDP provides a practical release pipeline with formal privacy guarantees and attack-based evaluation in the tested settings.
}
\end{quote}

\paragraph{Why this abstract is better}
The present abstract in the manuscript is already improved, but it still carries too many ideas at once, including Shannon/Renyi entropy, histogram calibration, synthetic-data release, Wiener kernel/RKHS, and practical significance, in a way that still feels slightly overloaded. A stronger TIFS-style abstract should foreground the main problem, the main mechanism, the formal guarantee, and the tested empirical outcome in a more focused way. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

\subsection*{C. Recommended Replacement Conclusion}

\begin{quote}
\textbf{
We have proposed REAEDP, a differential privacy framework for entropy-calibrated data release with formal guarantees and attack-based evaluation.
The main technical contributions are twofold:
first, an explicit sensitivity bound for histogram entropy (Theorem~3), which enables calibrated differentially private release of histogram statistics;
second, a synthetic-data release mechanism \(F\) with a formal \((\varepsilon,\delta)\)-DP guarantee under the stated parameter domain (Theorem~4).
Across multiple public tabular datasets, the experiments show that the empirical entropy sensitivity remains below the theoretical bound in the tested regime, that standard Laplace and Gaussian histogram baselines achieve comparable behavior, and that membership-inference and linkage-style attack performance moves toward random-guess behavior as \(\varepsilon\) decreases.
These results support the use of REAEDP as an algorithmic release pipeline for privacy-preserving data publication in the tested settings.

This work also has several limitations.
The entropy-based release depends on histogram discretization and may be sensitive to bin selection, especially in high-dimensional settings.
Scalability to very high-dimensional or very large-scale data may require approximate or distributed implementations.
The RKHS/Wiener-kernel analysis relies on finite-dimensional numerical approximation of an infinite-dimensional space, and tightening the associated approximation error under differential privacy remains open.
Finally, while the paper explicitly evaluates membership inference and a linkage-style attack, stronger experimental coverage of attribute inference and broader multimodal or federated settings is left for future work.
}
\end{quote}

\paragraph{Why this conclusion is better}
Your current conclusion is already better than earlier versions because it has removed some of the strongest overclaims, but it still retains wording such as \emph{under the Wiener kernel space}, and it still gives somewhat equal rhetorical weight to all subcomponents of the paper. The paper is currently strongest when the conclusion emphasizes the entropy bound, the synthetic mechanism \(F\), and the tested attack behavior, then treats RKHS and broader extensions as secondary or future-facing.
